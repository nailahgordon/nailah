In Plato's Cave – Reflection

Reflection on Plato’s Cave and Photography Plato’s Allegory of the Cave is about prisoners who have only ever seen shadows on a wall. They think the shadows are real because that’s all they know. When one prisoner is freed and sees the real world outside the cave, he realizes that the shadows were just a small part of reality. This story made me think about how people often accept what they see without questioning it. It connects to Susan Sontag’s On Photography, where she talks about how photos shape the way we understand the world. Photographs, like the shadows, can give us a limited view of reality. Sontag explains that photographs let us capture moments and remember them, but they don’t tell the whole story. A photo shows only what the camera focuses on and leaves out everything else. This is similar to the prisoners in the cave, who see only shadows and assume they are the truth. Reading her made me realize that we need to think about what is missing in what we see. Photos can be meaningful and powerful, but they can also make us believe we understand something fully when we only see one side. Plato’s story also shows that learning and awareness take effort. The freed prisoner has to get used to the sunlight and learn to understand what is really happening outside the cave. Sontag challenges readers to do the same with photographs: we have to look carefully and question them instead of just accepting them. A photo might show something dramatic or sad, but to really understand, we need to think about the context and what isn’t shown. Sontag also points out that photos can make people passive. We look at images of war, poverty, or events far away, and we might feel sympathy, but we are not part of the reality behind the picture. This reminded me of the prisoners who only watch shadows. We can see images and think we know something, but we are only seeing part of it. To truly understand, we have to go beyond the surface and think critically, just like the freed prisoner has to adjust to the real world. In the end, Plato’s cave and Sontag’s ideas both show that what we see is not always the full truth. Shadows on the wall and photographs can trick us into thinking we understand reality. This reflection helped me realize that it’s important to question what we see, look deeper, and think about what might be missing. Reality is more complicated than any one image or moment, and both Plato and Sontag remind us to stay curious and careful about how we perceive the world.
